Creativity Toolkit II: Creative Collaboration Notes

This module focuses on how to foster creativity within teams, challenging common assumptions about what makes a team effective. It covers team composition, the impact of different personality types, the role of individualism versus collectivism, the importance of dissent, the benefits of competition, and how culture influences creative outcomes.

Module 4: Creative Collaboration

4-1 Leveraging Social Influence

Problem with Team Creativity: Teams often establish norms that work against creativity, leading to blind conformity, obedience, silence, and overconfidence.

Solution: Norms can be flipped to facilitate the creative process.

Key Concept: Team Composition: The mix of personalities within a team is crucial for creative output. It's not always ideal to have only highly creative people.

  • Creative Personalities: Tend to be rebellious, non-conforming, and unpredictable. While good for generating ideas, they may need to be "reigned in" for other aspects of the creative process.
  • Conformist Personalities: People willing to adopt the group's point of view, ignoring their own.
  • Attention to Detail Personalities: People who focus narrowly on tiny details and strive for perfection.

Ideal Personality Mix: Primarily creative people, but with one or two conformists.

  • Creatives' Role: Enhance conflict and integrate diverse perspectives.
  • Conformists' Role: Provide structure and efficient execution, acting as the "glue" for the team.

Important Concept: Diminishing Creativity: Excessive focus on tiny details to the exclusion of the big picture can diminish creativity. Having too many detail-focused individuals can be counterproductive.

Example (Google): A former Google designer noted that teams of engineers would obsess over minute details like the shade of blue for a logo (considering 41 shades) or border width (four or five pixels). This obsessive attention to detail was paralyzing, preventing the team from taking risks and exploring creative directions.

Surprising Trait: Narcissism: While often seen negatively (self-aggrandizing, self-absorbed, overconfident, aggressive to criticism), having one or two narcissists on a team can actually boost creativity.

  • Evidence (Goncalo, Flynn, & Kim, 2010 study):
    • Teams with zero narcissists were efficient but produced boring, uninteresting ideas, agreeing too quickly.
    • Teams with all narcissists was a complete disaster because they couldn't even agree on which idea to pursue.
    • Sweet Spot: Teams with a 50-50 mix of narcissists were the most creative.
    • Reason: Narcissists try to stand out by suggesting outlandish ideas, breaking the team's fear of risk-taking and controversial ideas, and inspiring others to take more risks.

Key Concept: Membership Change: Even with the right personality mix, teams can get stale. Rotating members introduces new knowledge and challenges existing norms/habits, boosting creativity.

4-2 Individualism

Key Concept: Norms: Habits that groups develop over time, shaping behavior and defining what's appropriate. They can constrain creativity by making everyone behave similarly.

Norms that Foster Creativity: Specific norms, like those encouraging individualism, can facilitate idea expression.

Individualism vs. Collectivism (Cultural Context):

  • Individualistic Cultures (e.g., US): Encourage uniqueness, pursuit of personal goals (even if at odds with team goals), and expression of personal points of view (even if it leads to conflict).
  • Collectivistic Cultures (e.g., China): Encourage focusing on commonalities, pursuing group goals (even if it means setting aside personal interests), and maintaining harmony (suppressing conflict).

Danger of Collectivism: While efficient and harmonious, it can "stamp out some of the behavior in teams necessary for creativity."

Study on Individualism and Team Creativity: Researchers primed people to think as individualists or collectivists before a brainstorming task about repurposing a vacant campus restaurant space.

  • Individualist Prime: Asked to think about what makes them unique, describe themselves, and why standing out is beneficial.
  • Collectivist Prime: Asked to think about commonalities, groups they belong to, and why blending in is advantageous.
  • Results:
    • Collectivists: Converged quickly on the least controversial, often more mundane idea (e.g., another restaurant), to avoid conflict and maintain harmony.
    • Individualists: More likely to argue and integrate ideas, wanting their own ideas reflected in the final product. This led to more intriguing, unusual, and creative ideas (Example: a museum of communist history with a cafe and a library).

Highlight: Individualism promoted a "second round of creativity" during the idea selection process that collectivistic groups didn't experience.

Process Difference: Individualists were more likely to "one-up each other," breaking from common thought and pursuing different lines of thinking. Collectivists had trouble breaking from their initial narrow focus.

Important Concept: The process leading to creativity may not always feel pleasant or cooperative. Individualistic teams might feel less "happy" but produce more creative outcomes.

Conclusion: While the world values "team players" (collectivism), for creativity, individualism is necessary as it encourages people to stand out, be different, assert uniqueness, engage in conflict, argue, and rebel – breaking barriers to creative expression.

4-3 Norms that Permit Dissent

Problem: People tend to conform and ignore their own perspectives, even when the majority is wrong, due to fear of not being liked or rocking the boat. This hinders creativity by withholding novel ideas.

Solution: Authentic Dissent: When expressed confidently and consistently, dissent can break conformity and bring more perspectives to the table.

Evidence (Supreme Court Study by Deborah Gruenfeld):

  • Studied decision-making processes of Supreme Court justices over 20 years.
  • Challenged the idea that liberals were inherently more creative than conservatives, recognizing that conservatives were often in the minority.
  • Finding: The status of being in the minority (dissenting) was more predictive of creative thinking than political ideology. When a justice was in the minority, regardless of liberal or conservative views, they engaged in more complex, flexible, and original thinking, leading to more creative solutions.

Highlight: Dissent sharpens thinking, makes people curious, and "wakes us up." It breaks false consensus and stimulates more people to think.

Important Note: Dissent must be authentic; people must truly endorse their point of view for the majority to pay attention.

Historical Example: "The Devil's Advocate": Dating back to Pope Sixtus V in 1587, the "Promotor of the Faith" was tasked with creating reasons against a person's sainthood to uncover flaws or misinterpretations of evidence. This institutionalized dissent to ensure thorough review.

4-4 Norms that Permit Competition

Challenge to Traditional Management: While cooperation is usually valued, competition can be advantageous for creativity.

Problem with Teams: Social Loafing: People can get lazy in teams, comparing themselves to the least productive and adjusting their effort downward, leading to less effort and fewer ideas.

Solution: Incentives to Compete: Encouraging competition can counteract social loafing.

Key Concept: Reward Distribution Rules:

  • Equality Rule: Everyone receives the same portion of the reward regardless of individual contribution.
  • Equity Rule: Rewards are proportionate to contributions (people who work harder get more).

Study on Equity vs. Equality Rule:

  • Teams assigned to the equity rule behaved more competitively, cutting each other off and trying to outdo each other.
  • These competitive behaviors led to more creative output, as people tried to "one-up each other" by sharing more ideas and more creative ideas.

Highlight: Competition, though not always pleasant or "pretty," can drive the exchange of creative ideas and full participation.

Important Fit: The effectiveness of competition (e.g., equity rule) depends on the team's composition.

  • Individualists: See competition as an opportunity to stand out and are motivated by it.
  • Collectivists: May find competition scary as they prefer social connections and harmony (Example: "the nail that sticks out gets hammered down"). This approach is likely to backfire with them.

Conclusion: There's a "fit" between the norm (e.g., equity rule) and the team's personality composition.

4-5 The Strong Culture Debate

Debate: Should a team aiming for creativity have an "anarchy" where people feel free to do whatever they want, or a "strong culture" that enforces norms like individualism, dissent, and competition?

Answer: It depends on the composition of the group.

  • Highly Creative People:
    • Naturally rebellious, questioning, competitive, and inclined to stand out.
    • No need for strong norms: They will naturally behave in ways appropriate for creativity.
    • Forcing norms can backfire: Strong norms can make them react against expectations or do nothing.
  • Less Creative Personalities:
    • Tend to shy away from behaviors needed in a creative team (reluctant to stand out or question things, more cooperative and obedient).
    • Strong norms can be leveraged: By specifying expectations (e.g., individualism) and applying social sanctions, less creative people can be encouraged toward more creative behavior.

Highlight: Creativity can be achieved even with "relatively ordinary people" by using social control and culture as a tool.

Overall: A contingent approach to managing creativity is needed, based on who is on your team.

4-6 Breaking Barriers and Being Creative in Teams

Review of Barriers to Team Creativity: Fear, silence, and overconfidence.

Addressing Fear (of social rejection):

  • Culture of Individualism: Emboldens people to stand out, be different, be unique, and engage in conflict if it moves the discussion forward. This lessens the tendency to conform and increases willingness to challenge the majority.
  • Highlight: Individualism can act as a buffer against social rejection concerns; individualists may even become more creative after being rejected, reframing it as simply being "different" and "not understood."

Addressing Silence:

  • Dissent: When someone confidently expresses disagreement, it can embolden others to speak up, minimizing silence.
  • Membership Change: Newcomers, not yet socialized to existing norms, can question established practices, signaling to the group that change is possible and encouraging others to speak.

Addressing Overconfidence:

  • Dissent: A confident dissenter forces the majority to re-examine their assumptions, encouraging reflection and a deeper understanding of their own positions.
  • Conflict: Sharpens thought and focuses on complexity, replacing superficial agreement with confidence based on sound reasoning and information.
4-7 Course Wrap-up

Big Picture: Creativity does not occur in a vacuum. It happens in a social context. Traditional team advice (e.g., "team player," cooperation, harmony) often works against creativity.

Key Takeaways for Fostering Creativity:

  • Embrace Individualism, Competition, and Dissent: These norms, though sometimes unpleasant, encourage people to stand out, be different, assert uniqueness, and challenge ideas.
  • Challenge Social Niceties and Coordination Focus: Instead of just getting in line and moving smoothly, focus on putting different viewpoints together and pushing each other.
  • Value the "Filtered Out" Individuals: Rebels, deviants, rejects, and defiant people are often necessary to slow down superficial processes and force deeper thinking.
  • Strategic Pause: Teams need to pause and challenge existing perspectives rather than immediately taking action. This "holding up" is crucial for finding different ways of doing things.
  • Active Culture Creation: Norms that support creativity don't arise naturally; they require hard work to specify and influence people to adopt them.
  • Navigate Idea Selection and Evaluation: Avoid picking ideas based on who suggested them or comfort. Separate the person from the idea, define clear criteria, and be open to changing criteria to make the most of new ideas.
  • Master Social Influence: Creative ideas, being unique, often generate uncertainty and negative initial reactions. Pitching effectively requires crafting communication to encourage perspective change and value new ideas.
