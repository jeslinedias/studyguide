Creativity Toolkit II: Creative Collaboration

Module 3: Team Dynamics and Creativity

Exploring the dynamics of creativity within teams, highlighting both their potential and the common barriers that hinder creative output.

Lesson 3-1: The Case for Teams

Individual Creativity vs. Team Creativity

Concept: While individual creativity focuses on changing personal perspectives to generate insights, team creativity involves connecting minds and sharing/changing perspectives among group members.

Point to Remember:

Intuitively, people assume that putting a group together and sharing perspectives should easily lead to creativity, but this is often not the case.

The Pervasiveness of Teams

Concept: Teams are ubiquitous in modern organizations, and individuals are spending more time working in teams than alone.

Point to Remember:

The ability to be a "team player" is sometimes valued above knowledge, skill, and ability in the workplace, which can be a disturbing trend.

The Post-It Note Story: A Case for Collective Creativity

Traditional View: The invention of the Post-It Note by Art Fry is often seen as a classic story of individual persistence and genius—a failed glue becoming a success.

Re-evaluated View (Team Creativity): The true story reveals a collective process:

  • Failed Glue: The original "failed" glue recipe existed for decades at 3M, stored due to a culture that valued failure.
  • Context and Culture: 3M had a culture that valued failure and maintained a vault of failed recipes, making it possible for the forgotten glue to be rediscovered.
  • Interdepartmental Collaboration: Art Fry learned about the failed glue through a lunchtime conversation with colleagues who remembered the recipe.
  • Unmet Need: Fry, a choir singer, identified a personal need for a removable bookmark.
  • Multiple Steps & Knowledge Sources: The invention involved various steps and different sources of knowledge, from glue creation and storage to identifying a need, funding a prototype, and marketing.

Points to Remember:

  • Complex problems requiring diverse strategies and approaches are where teams excel.
  • Teams allow for the combination of different sources of knowledge (e.g., glue maker, recipe rememberer, user with unmet need, administrative assistants for testing).
  • It is easier and more realistic to rely on teams to distribute knowledge and tasks rather than finding one "lone genius."

Lesson 3-2: Teams Put Up Barriers

Barriers to Speaking Up in Teams

Concept: While teams bring diverse ideas, these ideas are useless if people are unwilling to voice them.

Conversational Bottleneck

Concept: In face-to-face team discussions, individuals must wait their turn to speak, leading to a bottleneck.

Impact: People may forget their ideas while waiting, or the conversation may move on, rendering their ideas irrelevant. This results in a loss of valuable ideas that would emerge if individuals worked alone.

Point to Remember:

Simply allocating more time does not solve this; listening to others can prevent individuals from generating their own ideas.

Solution: Encourage individuals to think and write down their ideas *before* the meeting.

Social Loafing (Free Riding)

Concept: In groups, individuals may feel they can "hide out" or rely on others, leading to reduced individual effort and fewer ideas contributed. "Many hands make light work" can lead to laziness.

Impact: Less raw material for creative solutions is generated. Social comparison can exacerbate this, with individuals feeling "fine" if they've contributed more than a perceived "loser."

Solution:

Make individual output visible and tracked. For example, using separate columns for each person's ideas on a brainstorming sheet can boost idea generation by as much as 30%.

Timidity and Fear of Judgment

Concept: People are often reluctant to share bold or unusual ideas because they fear negative judgment, criticism, or appearing foolish, stupid, irrelevant, or crazy.

Impact: Individuals often share only their safest ideas, avoiding risk-taking necessary for groundbreaking ideas. This is less of an issue when working alone.

Point to Remember:

Teams can make people timid, blocking the free exchange of perspectives required for creativity.

Potential Solution: Some groups explicitly rule out criticism to encourage sharing, though this is a simpler problem to solve compared to others.

Lesson 3-3: Uniformity, Silence, and Social Control: How Teams Kill Creativity

Lesson 3-3.1: Conformity

Asch Conformity Experiment

Concept: This classic experiment demonstrated the power of groups to exert conformity pressure, leading individuals to go against the evidence of their own senses and adopt a majority viewpoint, even when clearly wrong.

Example: Participants were asked to match the length of lines; a significant percentage gave the wrong answer at least once, conforming to a visibly incorrect majority.

Reasons for Conformity

  • Informational Influence: The assumption that "truth lies in numbers"; if everyone else agrees, they must be right, or they know something you don't. This acts as a heuristic (rule of thumb).
  • Normative Influence: The desire to fit in, be liked, and avoid appearing deviant or causing conflict.

Impact on Creativity: Conformity encourages coordination and getting along, but not necessarily creativity, which requires different perspectives.

Solutions to Reduce Conformity Pressure:

  • Allow dissenters to speak first.
  • Do not make the majority view known initially.
  • Once individuals have publicly committed to a viewpoint, they are more likely to stick to it, even if it contradicts the group.
  • Allow lower-status individuals to speak first to prevent them from conforming to higher-status members.

Lesson 3-3.2: Blind Obedience

Hierarchy and Idea Suppression

Concept: Teams easily form hierarchies (based on power or status), where those at the top can impose their will, and ideas from lower levels may not be considered legitimate or worth sharing.

Impact on Creativity: Hierarchies can lead to blind obedience rather than rebellious, non-conforming, or questioning behaviors essential for creative perspectives.

Milgram Experiment (Study of Learning)

Concept: This classic study demonstrated the universal tendency to obey authority, even when it involves potentially harmful actions.

Example: Participants, acting as "teachers," were instructed by an experimenter to deliver increasing electric shocks to a "learner" (an actor) for incorrect answers. Nearly 90% of participants delivered the highest, supposedly lethal, voltage when ordered to continue by the experimenter.

Relevance to Creativity: Teams provide a context where this blind obedience can occur, with hierarchies enabling those at the top to suppress diverse viewpoints and promote conformity instead of creativity.

Middle-Status Conformity

Concept: The pressure to conform is often strongest for individuals in the middle of a hierarchy. They tend to be reluctant to voice creative ideas and may try to preserve the status quo.

Point to Remember:

Stereotypes of middle managers as narrow-minded bureaucrats who shoot down new ideas have a "kernel of truth."

Lesson 3-3.3: Polarization

Concept: Groups can become more extreme in their initial viewpoints rather than engaging in thoughtful deliberation or rethinking perspectives.

Impact: This "mindless" polarization is detrimental to creativity, especially in consequential decisions (e.g., juries, politics) where thoughtful consideration is paramount. Instead of being creative, groups may simply retrench into initial positions.

Lesson 3-3.4: Feeling Like a Deviant When You're Not

Concept: Individuals in teams can misperceive what others think, leading them to feel like a "deviant" for holding a certain viewpoint, even if many others secretly agree.

Impact: This creates an "atmosphere of silence" where people are unwilling to express authentic points of view or break perceived norms. Consequently, critical information or alternative perspectives are withheld, and mistaken perceptions persist.

Example: In classrooms, students often remain silent when asked if they have questions, even if they are confused, because no one else is raising their hand.

Point to Remember:

Teams can unintentionally promote silence rather than free expression.

Lesson 3-3.5: Common Knowledge Effect

Concept: When diverse groups are formed with the intention of sharing unique knowledge, they tend to focus on information they already have in common rather than the unique information each member possesses.

Reasons:

  • Common information is shared quickly and easily, forming an immediate basis for group perspective.
  • Sharing unique information that contradicts an emerging consensus can feel awkward, and individuals are reluctant to do so due to conformity pressures.
  • Even if unique information is voiced, it may be ignored or dismissed by others because it doesn't fit the established conversation or is not reinforced by other members.

Impact: The primary benefit of forming a diverse group—the exchange of unique perspectives and information—is lost, hindering creativity.

Lesson 3-3.6: Over-confident Teams

Concept: Groups can become overconfident, believing they have the right answer even if the process that led them there was flawed. This often happens when groups agree quickly, suppress doubts, and reinforce shared information.

Collective Efficacy (Positive Aspect): Confidence can motivate effort, lead to setting difficult goals, and increase persistence and resilience.

Negative Impact of Overconfidence:

  • Teams may persist in the wrong direction, leading to eventual failure.
  • Early confidence can lead teams to delay crucial meetings and avoid necessary conflicts (e.g., debating project topics, assigning roles).
  • These suppressed issues can resurface later when it's too late to address them effectively.

Point to Remember:

Overconfidence, especially if developed too early or based on a poor understanding, can suppress the exchange of perspectives vital for creativity.

Lesson 3-3.7: Why Do We Feel Creative in Teams?

Concept: Despite the numerous barriers, people often *feel* more creative when working in teams, even if the actual creative output is lower than individual work.

Reasons for this Perception:

  • Social Enjoyment: Team interactions are often fun, engaging, and provide social connection (laughter, conversation). This enjoyment can be confused with actual creativity.
  • Perceived Buy-in/Commitment: Being part of the discussion can lead to a feeling of ownership and commitment to the outcome, regardless of the creative process.
  • Reinforcement of Ideas: Team members often complement each other's ideas and provide positive feedback ("That's a great idea!"), which boosts individual confidence and the perception of collective creativity, even if critical evaluation is lacking.
  • Collective Memory: Teams can help in remembering solutions or ideas that might have been forgotten if individuals worked alone, as seen in the Post-It Note example.
  • "Team Player" Mentality: The organizational emphasis on being a "team player" can lead to a culture where criticism is avoided and support is prioritized, which feels good but might not foster true creative challenge.

Point to Remember:

The positive social experience of teamwork can mask a lack of genuine creative output, making teams seem more creative than they are. This discrepancy between perception and reality makes it challenging to address the underlying issues that hinder team creativity.
