4. What to Play?

Multinational Enterprises (MNEs) must balance two fundamental pressures: the need to reduce costs and the need to be locally responsive. The interplay of these pressures defines four primary global strategies. Click on any quadrant of the chart to see details.

Access Larger Markets

The most common driver for internationalization is the pursuit of growth. By entering new countries, firms can significantly expand their potential customer base beyond the confines of their domestic market.

Examples: Companies target economically developed regions like OECD countries or high-growth emerging markets like China and India, where a rising middle class creates new demand. For instance, China now accounts for a massive share of global luxury brand sales.

Access Better or Lower-Cost Factors of Production

Firms often go abroad to find more efficient or cheaper inputs for their production processes. This can provide a significant cost advantage.

Examples: This includes cheaper **labor** (e.g., iRobot manufacturing in China), access to **natural resources** (e.g., the oil industry), or specialized **technology and talent** (e.g., setting up an IT development center in India).

Reduce Overall Business Risk

By diversifying operations geographically, companies can insulate themselves from the economic downturns, political instability, or market saturation in any single country.

Concept: This is a classic diversification strategy applied to geography. If the home market struggles, strong performance in other regions can stabilize the company's overall financial health.

Leverage Core Capabilities

International expansion allows firms to get more mileage out of their existing strengths and proprietary assets.

Examples: Companies with strong brands, patented technology, or efficient operational models (like McDonald's or Yum! Brands' franchise systems) can deploy these capabilities in new markets to create value.

Develop New Capabilities

Operating abroad is a powerful learning opportunity. Firms can gain new skills and insights that can enhance their global competitiveness.

Examples: A firm might develop expertise in managing complex supply chains by operating in Southeast Asia, or it could learn about new consumer trends from a sophisticated market like Japan. John Deere's expansion into India forces it to learn how to compete in a market dominated by smaller tractors, developing new product capabilities.

International Strategy

Position: Low pressure for cost reduction, low pressure for local responsiveness.

This strategy involves taking products first produced for the domestic market and selling them internationally with only minimal local customization. It's often the first step for companies venturing abroad.

Benefits: Leverages existing core competencies, relatively simple to implement, can use low-risk entry modes like exporting.

Risks: Limited local responsiveness can lead to missed market opportunities, vulnerability to exchange rates, and risk of IP theft.

Examples: Rolex, Harley-Davidson, Starbucks (initially).

Localization (Multidomestic) Strategy

Position: Low pressure for cost reduction, high pressure for local responsiveness.

This strategy prioritizes tailoring products and services to fit local consumer preferences and host-country requirements. The goal is to act like a domestic company in each market.

Benefits: Maximizes local relevance and market share, reduces exchange rate exposure.

Risks: High cost due to duplication of business functions, forfeits economies of scale, can have limited learning across different country divisions.

Examples: Nestlé, which customizes its food products extensively for different markets.

Global-Standardization Strategy

Position: High pressure for cost reduction, low pressure for local responsiveness.

This strategy focuses on reaping cost reductions from economies of scale, learning effects, and location economies. The MNE offers a standardized product worldwide.

Benefits: Achieves low-cost position through global-scale efficiencies and division of labor.

Risks: Lack of local responsiveness, little product differentiation, potential for a "race to the bottom" on wages and standards.

Examples: Lenovo, Infosys, and Tesla with its Gigafactories aimed at standardized global production.

Transnational Strategy

Position: High pressure for cost reduction, high pressure for local responsiveness.

The most complex strategy, it attempts to achieve both low costs through global-scale efficiency and high local responsiveness. It requires fostering a global learning environment where knowledge and best practices flow between headquarters and subsidiaries.

Benefits: "Best of both worlds" – combines global efficiencies with local adaptation, fosters global learning.

Risks: Very difficult to implement, complex and costly matrix structures can lead to internal confusion and conflicting goals.

Examples: P&G, John Deere, Haier, which standardizes internal components but customizes appliance designs for different markets.
