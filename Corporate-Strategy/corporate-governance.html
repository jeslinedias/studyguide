Core Frameworks

Understanding the foundational concepts of stakeholders and social responsibility.

The Stakeholder Universe

A company's success depends on balancing the diverse interests of its internal and external stakeholders. Shareholder primacy, a recent trend, argues for prioritizing investor returns, but this view is increasingly being challenged.

Internal
  • Managers: Individuals responsible for the day-to-day operations and strategic direction.
  • Shareholders: Owners of the company's stock, seeking financial returns.
  • Employees: Individuals who work for the company, contributing labor and expertise.
  • Board Members: Individuals elected to oversee the company's management and represent stakeholder interests.
External
  • Customers: Individuals or organizations who purchase the company's products or services.
  • Suppliers: Organizations that provide raw materials, components, or services to the company.
  • Governments: Regulatory bodies that set laws and policies affecting the company.
  • Communities: Local populations and environments affected by the company's operations.

Governance in Action

Exploring the mechanisms that direct and control a firm, and the challenges that arise.

The Agency Dilemma

Agency Theory examines the problems that arise when one party (the agent, e.g., a manager) is expected to act in the best interests of another (the principal, e.g., a shareholder). This separation of ownership and control can lead to two key issues due to information asymmetry.

The Role of the Board

The Board of Directors is the centerpiece of corporate governance, tasked with general oversight and a range of critical duties to ensure the company is run effectively and ethically.

  • ✓Select, evaluate, and compensate the CEO.
  • ✓Oversee CEO succession planning.
  • ✓Approve major strategic initiatives.
  • ✓Conduct risk assessment and mitigation.
  • ✓Ensure financial statement accuracy and legal compliance.

Modern Trends & Challenges

How corporations are adapting to new pressures and opportunities.

The Rise of "Ecosystem" Strategies

The term "ecosystem" is increasingly used to describe modern business environments. This shift from an industry view to a community view (including suppliers, customers, and complementors) emphasizes cooperation and is particularly vital for platform-based businesses like Apple, Amazon, and Coursera.

Shielding from Market Pressure

To counter short-term market pressures, companies use various tactics to protect their long-term vision. However, these methods carry their own risks.

A defense tactic making a hostile takeover prohibitively expensive. Example: In 2012, Netflix's board adopted a poison pill to thwart pressure from activist investor Carl Icahn.
Taking a company private or remaining a private "Unicorn" (startup valued >$1B) shields it from public market scrutiny, leading to a boom in private equity.
Founders retain superior voting rights to protect long-term vision. Cautionary Tale: WeWork's founder, Adam Neumann, held such shares, leading to a massive buyout by investor SoftBank after the company's spectacular collapse, highlighting the risks of unchecked founder control.

Broadening the Mission

Companies are adopting tools like the Balanced Scorecard to create a more holistic view of performance beyond short-term financials, incorporating customer, operational, and organizational metrics.

Financial: Profitability, growth
Customer: Differentiation, cost
Operations: Products, fulfillment
Organizational: Learning, leadership
