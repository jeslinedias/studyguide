What is Corporate Strategy?

Corporate strategy is defined as "the pursuit of competitive advantage through the configuration and coordination of a company's multi-business activities." It involves crucial decisions and actions concerning:

  • The scope of the firm
  • Coordinating synergies across businesses
  • Corporate transactions

Module 1 focuses primarily on the vertical and horizontal scope of companies, exploring choices related to vertical integration and diversification.

Lesson 1-1: Introduction: Corporate Scope

What is Corporate Scope?

Simply put, corporate scope refers to the "footprint" of the company â€“ which activities or businesses a company is engaged in, and how should they be managed.

Three Dimensions of Corporate Scope:

  • Vertical Scope (Vertical Integration): What stages of the value chain (or network) should the firm operate in?
  • Horizontal Scope (Diversification): What range of products and services should the firm offer?
  • Geographic Scope: Where in the world does the firm compete? (Note: This module focuses primarily on vertical and horizontal scope, with geographic scope covered in the International Strategy module.)

These are real choices companies make. For example:

  • Vertical Scope Example: PepsiCo (2009) and Coca-Cola (2010) acquiring their bottlers is an example of forward integration, adding a new stage to their value chain.
  • Horizontal Scope Example: GE's shift in focus from financial services (GE Capital), media (NBC), and appliances to power systems, aviation, and renewable energy under CEOs Jack Welch and Larry Culp, demonstrates changing horizontal scope.

Lesson 1-2: Vertical Scope Part 1

Defining Vertical Scope and Vertical Integration

When discussing vertical scope, two related questions arise:

  • Which parts of the value chain (or value network) should a company operate in? (A business strategy question: which part is more profitable?)
  • Should the same company be integrated across specific stages of the value chain (or network)? (The classic vertical integration question: does managing these parts within the same company create more value than if they were separate?)

Vertical Integration Terminology:

The value chain illustrates primary and support activities:

  • Forward Integration: Moving closer to the customer (e.g., a producer getting into distribution and sales). PepsiCo acquiring bottlers is a forward integration example.
  • Backward Integration: Moving closer to raw materials (e.g., a manufacturer getting into component production or raw material sourcing).
  • Outsourcing: A vertical integration choice, such as deciding whether to build an IT system internally or outsource it.

Example: University of Illinois and Coursera (Outsourcing)

The University of Illinois contracts with Coursera to host its online courses. This is an outsourcing decision.

Motivations for Outsourcing:

  • Lack of Resources/Capabilities: Outsourcing when the company lacks the internal quality or capabilities (e.g., University of Illinois lacking Coursera's software skills). This can be self-reinforcing, as the partner keeps learning.
  • Ability to Aggregate Demand (Scale): Partner firm can achieve greater scale (e.g., Coursera serving many universities, Walmart aggregating consumer demand).
  • Responsiveness to Market/Technology Trends: Specialized external firms are often more responsive and at the cutting edge.

Motivations for Vertical Integration:

  • Market Power: Creating entry barriers by controlling a key resource (though often limited by competition and antitrust laws).
  • Improving Quality/Cost: More control over operations can lead to better quality or cost efficiency.
  • Planning, Coordinating, Control: Easier to plan and coordinate operations when owned internally.
  • Investments in Specialized Assets: Critical if the supplier needs to make investments specific to the buyer, which can lead to a "hold-up problem."

Lesson 1-3: Vertical Scope Part 2: Transaction Cost Economics (TCE)

Transaction Cost Economics (TCE) / Theory of the Firm

Transaction Cost Economics (TCE), primarily associated with Nobel laureate Oliver Williamson, is a field of research that addresses when to vertically integrate. It deals with:

  • Transaction Costs: Costs associated with economic exchanges (e.g., negotiating, monitoring, enforcing contracts). More prevalent in market exchanges/outsourcing.
  • Administrative Costs: Costs associated with organizing activities within a hierarchy (e.g., bureaucracy, weak incentives, sclerotic response). More prevalent in vertical integration.

Comparative Organizational Analysis (Williamson's Key Insight)

Oliver Williamson's concept of Comparative Organizational Analysis is a central theme in understanding corporate scope. It emphasizes de-linking two critical issues when making organizational choices:

  • What is the objective? What specific benefits are we trying to achieve (e.g., market power, efficiencies, leveraging capabilities)?
  • What organizational form best achieves this objective? This involves comparing various governance modes, from market-like alternatives (outsourcing) to full integration (hierarchy).

Williamson's core mantra is: "All organizational forms are flawed." There is no single perfect structure. The key is to understand the inherent trade-offs.

  • Markets: Provide better adaptation and more autonomy. This means market mechanisms (like contracts with external suppliers) are generally more flexible and responsive to changes, and external parties retain greater independence.
  • Hierarchies (Firms/Integration): Provide better coordination and more control. When activities are integrated within a single firm, it allows for tighter management, easier information flow, and direct oversight.

As described by Williamson, moving along the spectrum of organizational modes:

  • Towards the "left" (less integrated, more market-like): We gain more autonomy and adaptation.
  • Towards the "right" (more integrated, more hierarchical): We gain more coordination and control.

The central task of corporate strategy, from Williamson's perspective, is to compare the advantages and disadvantages of these plausible alternatives and select the organizational form that best fits the specific transaction and its objectives.

Three Key Transaction Costs (when outsourcing):

  • Adverse Selection: Arises due to information asymmetries exists between firm and its transaction partner, say, a supplier.. Illustrated by George Akerlof's "Lemons Problem" (used car market: sellers know more about quality than buyers). A supplier might not deploy its best resources or technologies.
  • Moral Hazard: Arises due to information asymmetries (ex post - after the transaction) where a party has private, unmeasurable performance information leading to "abuse" of a benefit. (e.g., a dealer reducing service quality if only sales are measured).
  • Hold-Up Problem (Williamson): Occurs when one party gains an upper hand after the transaction due to asset specificity (investments specific to the other party, e.g., customized machinery, tied-in IT systems). This can lead to opportunism (self-interest seeking with guile). Also includes knowledge/technology leakage.

Three Key Administrative Costs (when integrating):

  • Weak Incentives: Internal units may be less focused on performance than external suppliers due to an expectation of continuity and being governed by fiat (orders within a permissible zone).
  • Principal-Agent Problem: Applies to owner-manager or manager-subordinate contexts. If performance is unobservable, the agent may underperform (akin to moral hazard but internal).
  • Lack of Dynamism: Vertically integrated hierarchies can be less responsive to market/technology trends due to bureaucracy and inability to "selectively intervene" (treat internal units like external suppliers).

Main Takeaway on Vertical Integration:

There's no general prescription for whether outsourcing or vertical integration is always better. The key is to align the attributes of the transaction with the appropriate governance mode.

  • Vertically integrate if: Investment in specific assets is needed, technology leakage hazards exist, or partner performance is difficult to measure.
  • Outsource if: Strong incentives for performance are needed, or more adaptation to market/technology trends is desired.

Lesson 1-4: Horizontal Scope Part 1

Horizontal Scope & Diversification

Similar to vertical scope, two questions arise for horizontal scope or diversification:

  • Which businesses should a company operate in? (Business strategy question)
  • Should the same company be integrated across a set of businesses? (Key corporate strategy question)

Diversification Example: University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign

Consider a multi-specialty university like the University of Illinois (with a business school as part of a larger university) versus an independent business school.

Advantages of being part of a larger university (Diversification):

  • Opportunity to connect with faculty and colleagues across campus for interdisciplinary learning and diverse perspectives.
  • Access to resources from other colleges (e.g., Engineering for technology/coding).
  • Larger student population leading to more diverse interactions and exposure to other fields.
  • Possibility for double majors in strong programs outside of business.

Disadvantages of being part of a larger university (Diversification):

  • Resources may be diverted to other university activities (e.g., service courses).
  • Students can feel "lost" in a large pool; resources are shared, not exclusive to business students.
  • Challenge in getting everyone working in the same direction due to broader enterprise goals.

Horizontal Scope Terminology:

  • Horizontal Mergers: In public policy discussions, this term refers to merging with another company in the exact same industry. This is about increasing the size of the company in the same business. For example, one automobile insurance company merging with another automobile insurance company would be a horizontal merger.
  • Diversification: More generally, when thinking of horizontal scope, it refers to the firm's footprint across a set of different, not the same, businesses. That is, the firm's diversification.
  • Related (and Unrelated) Diversification: These are common terminologies used within diversification, referring to whether the new businesses have underlying connections in their resources or capabilities.

Lesson 1-5: Horizontal Scope Part 2: The BCG Matrix and Conglomerates

History of Conglomerates

In the 1960s-1970s, conglomerates (collections of unrelated businesses) were common in the US. Their heyday ended in the 1980s, as depicted in the movie "Wall Street" with Gordon Gekko breaking them up. Gekko argued that these companies were mismanaged and destroying value due to bureaucracy and inefficiencies.

The BCG Growth-Share Matrix (1970s)

Developed by Boston Consulting Group for GE, the BCG Matrix helps manage a portfolio of businesses (Strategic Business Units or SBUs) at various stages of their industry life cycle.

The BCG Matrix categorizes SBUs based on their market growth and relative market share, helping companies decide where to invest, divest, or harvest resources.

Axes:

  • Vertical Axis: Market Growth / Industry Attractiveness (fast growing vs. slow growing)
  • Horizontal Axis: Relative Market Share / Company Strength (strong vs. weak presence)

The BCG Matrix maps a company's businesses (SBUs), represented by bubbles (larger bubbles indicate larger businesses by revenue). These SBUs evolve over time and are categorized into four types:

  • Dogs: Businesses with low market growth and low market share. These are neither in growing industries nor an area of strength. Strategy: Generally, these businesses would be divested to raise cash.
  • Question Marks: Businesses in fast-growing markets but with low market share, where the company has yet to establish a strong presence. The company must make a choice: invest to strengthen its position or divest the business.
  • Stars: SBUs with fast-growing markets and strong market share. These are considered the future of the company. Strategy: The company should be investing heavily in these SBUs for continued growth.
  • Cash Cows: Typically yesterday's stars, these are businesses where the company has a strong presence but in markets that are not growing quickly. There's less need to invest heavily, and they often yield excess cash due to the company's strong position. This cash can be redeployed elsewhere.

The strategic goal is to create a balanced portfolio of SBUs that balances current and future success with resource availability and needs. The core idea is to either divest businesses or invest in fast-growing ones to build strong positions. Eventually, successful Star businesses are expected to mature and become Cash Cows, generating cash for the next generation of Stars.

Effectively, conglomerates used this framework to act as their own bankers, transferring cash internally. This approach was particularly effective in an era when US financial markets were much less developed, a scenario still observed in some other countries today.

Case Study: ITT Corporation

ITT (International Telephone and Telegraph) diversified aggressively into unrelated businesses (insurance, hotels, car rentals) under CEO Harold Geneen, believing "it's all about managing by the numbers."

Decline of the Conglomerate Model:

  • General management knowledge was not enough; specialized knowledge became necessary.
  • Management capabilities became more common; conglomerates no longer offered unique value.
  • Corporate headquarters became bloated and inefficient.
  • Focused incentives in each business were muted.

ITT began selling off businesses in the 1980s. In 2011, it split into three parts: Xylem (water), Exelis (defense), and "new" ITT (engineered parts). The stock market rewarded this breakup with a ~20% increase, indicating that financial markets are more efficient at allocating cash than internal transfers in diversified firms, and diversified firms suffer from bureaucratic inefficiencies.

This phenomenon is known as a diversification discount.

Lesson 1-6: Horizontal Scope Part 3

Diversification and Performance

  • Diversification Discount: Unrelated diversification is generally associated with lower performance, meaning the value of the combined business is less than the sum of its parts. This aligns with Gordon Gekko's strategy of breaking up conglomerates.
  • Diversification Premium: The best performance is associated with related diversification, where the value of a diversified firm is greater than its SBUs separately. This is a common finding in corporate strategy research.

Motivations for Diversification (Value Creation)

The key to value-creating diversification lies in pursuing benefits that cannot be achieved as effectively by separate, independent firms.

  • Market Power: While a theoretical motivation (e.g., reducing competition through bundling or cross-subsidization), this is often problematic due to antitrust laws and practical challenges in implementation.
  • Synergies (Economies of Scope): These are the most critical motivations for diversification, involving complementarities between businesses that lead to value creation.
    • Resource Scaling (or Sharing): This occurs when a company uses its existing, non-rival resources (resources whose use by one business does not diminish their availability for another) across multiple businesses. This can create economies of scale at the corporate level.
      • Examples: Using a strong brand like Nike for both athletic shoes and apparel; leveraging Amazon's logistics infrastructure and IT platform for various retail ventures; applying general management skills across different business units (though this is increasingly specialized today).
      • Implication: Resource scaling often requires more coordination and more operating control of units because the shared resource needs active management to ensure efficient use and consistent quality across all leveraging businesses.
    • Resource Redeployment (or Transferring): This involves transferring unique, firm-specific resources from one business unit to another. These are typically underutilized or slack resources.
      • Examples: Uber initially leveraged its driver network and mapping technology for ride-sharing, then redeployed these resources to expand into Uber Eats (food delivery); a firm might transfer excess manufacturing capacity or highly specialized talent.
      • Implication: Resource redeployment generally allows for more autonomy and financial control of units because the resource is being moved or utilized in a new context, rather than being continuously shared across ongoing operations. Modern financial markets have made cash a less unique redeployable resource, as companies can often raise capital more efficiently externally.
  • Less Accepted Motivations:
    • Reducing Risks: Shareholders can diversify their portfolios more efficiently than a company can diversify its operations.
    • Empire Building: This often stems from managerial hubris or misaligned incentives, where managers pursue growth for personal gain rather than shareholder value.
    • Pursue Profitable Opportunities: If an attractive opportunity is completely unrelated to the core business, it is often optimal to spin it out early to maximize its value.

In summary, the core motivations for value-creating diversification are resource and capability-based synergies. Diversification works best when there are tangible benefits from combining businesses that arise from shared or transferred unique resources.

Comparative Organizational Analysis for Diversification

Apply Williamson's approach: De-link the objective from the organizational form. Consider market arrangements (alliances, contracts, licensing) as plausible alternatives to diversification.

John Deere's partnership with Hitachi for mining equipment and Honda/steel products in its dealerships demonstrates using alliances instead of direct diversification to achieve goals. This highlights that integration isn't always the best or only way to achieve synergies.

Implications for Horizontal Scope (Two Key Tests)

Diversification must satisfy two tests to be considered value-creating:

  • The Better-Off Test: Does the combination of businesses create value (do synergies exist)? This is the value creation test.
  • The Ownership Test: To access this value, does the same company need to own the businesses (hierarchy) or can it be done better with a market-like arrangement (e.g., contracts, alliances)? This is the value capture test.

Resources and Relatedness: Relatedness in diversification fundamentally implies relatedness in underlying firm-specific resources. Synergies, whether through scaling or redeployment, arise from these resources being leveraged across businesses, which often requires ownership to overcome market failures.

Lesson 1-7: Managing Integrated Firms

Broad Approach So Far:

  • Separate benefits sought from governance modes.
  • Integration as an alternative to "market forms."
  • Comparative organizational analysis.

Strategy Extends Beyond Make-Buy Decisions:

Why haven't PepsiCo and Coca-Cola always owned their bottlers? While integration can offer control and ensure investments in specific assets, independent bottlers offer better market understanding and incentives.

Therefore, companies must think creatively to maintain market-like features even in integrated operations (e.g., commissions for managers in bottling operations) or ensure goals are met through contracts in outsourced operations (e.g., mandatory training for customer-facing personnel).

Managing Diversified Units:

For diversified firms like Alphabet (Google) and Amazon, which have units that could be independent companies, the approach varies:

  • More Autonomous Approaches: Replicate market incentives (e.g., Google's Alphabet structure with Waymo as an independent subsidiary).
  • More Coordinated Approaches: Reduce transaction costs through hierarchical organization, especially when synergies are from resource scaling/sharing (e.g., Amazon integrating Whole Foods' IT systems for online grocery business, requiring active operational control).
  • Resource Redeployment: Allows for more autonomy and financial controls (e.g., GE's approach to managing managerial talent as a corporate resource).

A Spectrum of Organizational Modes:

There's a spectrum from full integration ("Make") to pure market modes ("Buy").

  • "Buy" (Less Integrated): Spot market transactions, short-term contracts. Offers better adaptation and autonomy.
  • Strategic Alliances (Middle): Relational contracts, licensing, franchising, equity alliances, joint ventures.
  • "Make" (More Integrated): Autonomous SBUs, Coordinated SBUs. Offers better coordination and control.

The choice between autonomous and coordinated alternatives within the firm depends on the nature of synergies.
